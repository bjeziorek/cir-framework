## CIR — Summary

**CIR is a five‑level model of cognitive stability that describes how access to emotional bandwidth, executive function, and behavioural control changes under stress or overload.**  
It provides a mechanistic, non‑clinical way to understand how cognitive resources fluctuate and how these fluctuations affect perception, reasoning, and decision‑making.

CIR serves as the **vertical stability axis** within my broader cognitive‑systems architecture (together with **FEDRI** and **SPINE**), supporting modelling of cognitive failure modes, emotional‑load dynamics, and behaviour under pressure.

The framework is used in:
- **cognitive‑inspired modelling**,  
- **human–system interaction**,  
- **stability‑aware system design**.

# CIR Framework

## Overview

CIR is a five‑level model of cognitive stability that describes how emotional bandwidth, executive function, and behavioural control fluctuate under stress, overload, or dysregulation. It was originally developed as an introspective framework and later became the stability axis within my broader cognitive‑systems architecture.

The model incorporates neuromodulatory dynamics (e.g., cortisol, dopamine, oxytocin, prolactin) and gating mechanisms that shape how cognitive resources are allocated under load.

CIR is used for modelling cognitive failure modes, emotional‑load dynamics, behavioural prediction, and stability‑aware system design.

CIR is not a diagnostic or therapeutic tool; it is a computational and mechanistic model of regulation that describes system dynamics rather than clinical conditions. The model has theoretical relevance for understanding patterns that appear in clinical contexts, but it is not a clinical tool and does not replace diagnosis or treatment.

---

## Origin

CIR originated in 2012 as a lightweight four‑level cognitive‑regulation model developed for a psychoeducational med‑tech concept in the form of an RPG‑style application. The system used narrative progression and in‑game feedback to help users map their own psychological state fluctuations and learn to recognise patterns in their regulation. The project was evaluated by Krakow Technology Park at 800,000 PLN (~$200,000) investment readiness, with a projected 6M PLN (~$1.5M) development budget.

The early version of CIR focused on modelling dysregulation states and did not yet include neuromodulatory or biochemical mechanisms. CIR‑5 (the enhanced‑stability state) was added shortly after the initial release, expanding the model beyond dysregulation mapping and establishing the full five‑level stability continuum. Over the following years, the framework evolved organically as new layers were added, including neuromodulation, gating mechanisms, and system‑level resource‑allocation dynamics.

Today’s CIR represents a significantly expanded and more computationally precise architecture than its original 2012 form, integrating cognitive dynamics, neuromodulation, and multi‑layered regulation processes into a unified stability model.

---

## Structure of the Model

CIR (Continuum · Introspection · Regulation) defines five regulation states ranging from severe dysregulation to enhanced stability. The model is not clinical; it is a mechanistic map of how cognitive states shift and how those shifts influence perception and decision‑making.

### CIR‑1 — Breakdown State (Severe Dysregulation)
A state of extreme destabilisation in which emotional regulation and behavioural control are essentially unavailable.

CIR‑1 is a breakdown state marked by complete loss of executive control and threat‑driven, fragmented cognition, where behaviour becomes purely reactive and functional decision‑making is impossible.

### CIR‑2 — Collapse State (High Dysregulation)
A state of significant instability in which control is fragile and easily collapses.

CIR‑2 is a high‑dysregulation collapse state in which executive control is unstable, information processing becomes emotion‑driven, and even mild stress can trigger withdrawal or shutdown.

### CIR‑3 — Fragile Stability State (Compensated Regulation)
A state of forced stabilisation in which functioning is possible but requires substantial effort.

CIR‑3 is a fragile‑stability state in which a person can function, but only through sustained cognitive effort, with limited flexibility and rapid fatigue.

### CIR‑4 — Stable Regulation State (Baseline Stability)
A state of healthy regulation in which functioning is smooth and balanced.

CIR‑4 is a baseline stability state in which regulation is smooth, executive functions are fully available, and cognition integrates emotion and logic without strain.

### CIR‑5 — Enhanced Stability State (Optimised Regulation)
A state of elevated stability in which the individual has access to full cognitive and emotional reserve.

CIR‑5 is an enhanced‑stability, peak‑performance state in which cognitive and emotional resources are fully available, enabling creative reasoning, strategic thinking, and highly adaptive stress regulation.

---

## Why CIR Matters

CIR makes it possible to design systems that don’t assume an ideal, always‑stable user, but instead account for real drops in cognitive stability and the resulting distortions in reasoning.

Most systems implicitly assume that users operate in stable states (Levels 3–4). In reality, people frequently drop to Levels 1–2 for short periods due to stress, frustration, fear, or cognitive overload. CIR provides a structured way to understand:

- why misinterpretations occur under pressure  
- how information processing changes across stability levels  
- when reasoning becomes constrained or distorted  
- how behaviour shifts during dysregulation  

This makes CIR a useful conceptual tool for analysing breakdowns and misunderstandings in human–system interactions.

# CIR Levels

CIR Levels define five functional states of a cognitive system under varying regulatory load. Each level reflects a distinct configuration of available cognitive resources, stability mechanisms, and information‑processing capacity. The model provides a structured way to analyse how systems behave when regulation weakens or strengthens — from breakdown states to optimised stability. Rather than describing traits, the levels map dynamic operating modes that inform system design, resilience engineering, and intervention strategies aimed at maintaining or restoring functional stability.

---

## CIR‑1 — Breakdown State (Severe Dysregulation)

**Cognitive resources** — minimal; primitive reactions dominate, no access to executive functions.  
**Behavioural control** — lost; impulsive, abrupt reactions, inability to inhibit responses.  
**Information processing** — fragmented and distorted; stimuli interpreted as threats.  
**Susceptibility to overload** — extreme; any additional stimulus worsens the state.  
**Interpretation patterns** — catastrophising, hostile attributions, threat perception even in neutral signals.  
**Typical behaviours** — aggression, self‑aggression, panic, sensory overreactivity; aggression and crying may co‑occur due to extreme sympathetic activation followed by a prolactin‑mediated reset.  
**Metacognition** — absent; no capacity to observe or modulate internal states.  
**Motivation / drive** — reactive impulses; no goal‑directed behaviour.  
**Characteristic cognitive biases** — hostile attribution bias, emotional reasoning, black‑and‑white threat perception.  
**Gating profile** — total gating collapse; inhibitory control fails, all inputs are threat‑tagged, and the system cannot filter or prioritise signals.  
**Neuromodulatory signature** — extremely high cortisol and noradrenaline with near‑zero serotonin; oxytocin absent; when overload becomes unsustainable, a prolactin‑driven distress reset is triggered after intense crying, enabling minimal recovery toward CIR‑2.  
**Memory bandwidth** — none; available memory is consumed by subconscious threat‑evaluation processes, with resources severely limited by HPA‑axis overload.  
**Functionality in the real world** — nearly impossible; the individual cannot perform tasks or make decisions.

---

## CIR‑2 — Collapse State (High Dysregulation)

**Cognitive resources** — limited; access to executive functions is intermittent.  
**Behavioural control** — fragile; short periods of control are possible but easily disrupted.  
**Information processing** — selective and emotion‑dominated; difficulty assessing situations.  
**Susceptibility to overload** — very high; overload leads to withdrawal or collapse.  
**Interpretation patterns** — fear‑based, pessimistic, threat‑oriented interpretations.  
**Typical behaviours** — crying, avoidance, freezing, apathy, low‑energy withdrawal, hypersensitivity.  
**Metacognition** — fragmented; occasional insight but no ability to act on it.  
**Motivation / drive** — avoidance‑based; withdrawal, shutdown, passive coping.  
**Characteristic cognitive biases** — catastrophising, overgeneralisation, negative filtering.  
**Gating profile** — unstable, oscillatory gating; the system alternates between hyper‑permeability (flooding) and shutdown (blocking).  
**Neuromodulatory signature** — elevated prolactin supporting a “grief‑reset” state; cortisol remains high but begins to plateau; serotonin remains low; oxytocin minimal unless relational cues appear.  
**Memory bandwidth** — minimal; most capacity is consumed by subconscious threat‑evaluation processes, with resources constrained by HPA‑axis overload.  
**Functionality in the real world** — significantly limited; simple tasks are possible but stability cannot be maintained.

---

## CIR‑3 — Fragile Stability State (Compensated Regulation)

**Cognitive resources** — moderate; executive functions operate but are energetically costly.  
**Behavioural control** — maintained through effort; regulatory fatigue occurs easily.  
**Information processing** — accurate but slowed; difficulty handling complex stimuli.  
**Susceptibility to overload** — moderate; overload triggers a drop to Level 2.  
**Interpretation patterns** — neutral or slightly negative; limited capacity for positive reinterpretation.  
**Typical behaviours** — functioning “on force”, reduced joy, sense of meaninglessness, survival‑mode patterns.  
**Metacognition** — present but effortful; self‑monitoring is possible but adjustment is difficult.  
**Motivation / drive** — task‑oriented but low‑energy; focus on obligations rather than goals.  
**Characteristic cognitive biases** — negative bias, reduced flexibility, rigid thinking.  
**Gating profile** — functional but high‑cost; inhibitory control is forced, resulting in slow, effortful regulation.  
**Neuromodulatory signature** — moderate cortisol with unstable dopamine; oxytocin rises only in safe relational contexts; serotonin partially recovers but remains fragile.  
**Memory bandwidth** — limited; cognitive resources remain constrained due to residual HPA‑axis load.  
**Functionality in the real world** — sufficient; the individual operates but without cognitive reserve.

---

## CIR‑4 — Stable Regulation State (Baseline Stability)

**Cognitive resources** — full; access to planning, reflection, and cognitive flexibility.  
**Behavioural control** — stable; reactions are appropriate and proportional.  
**Information processing** — balanced; emotions and logic integrate smoothly.  
**Susceptibility to overload** — low; stress is regulated adaptively.  
**Interpretation patterns** — realistic and balanced; capacity for positive and neutral interpretation.  
**Typical behaviours** — healthy sensitivity, appropriate boundaries, relational capacity, experience of joy.  
**Metacognition** — stable; the individual can observe, evaluate, and adjust internal states.  
**Motivation / drive** — consistent; ability to pursue goals and maintain routines.  
**Characteristic cognitive biases** — balanced appraisal, contextual reasoning, flexible thinking.  
**Gating profile** — efficient, selective gating; inhibitory and excitatory pathways balanced, enabling smooth filtering and flexible attention.  
**Neuromodulatory signature** — balanced dopamine and serotonin; stable oxytocin supports adaptive regulation, social engagement, and cognitive flexibility.  
**Memory bandwidth** — full; cognitive resources are fully available.  
**Functionality in the real world** — full; the individual functions effectively and stably.

---

## CIR‑5 — Enhanced Stability State (Optimised Regulation)

**Cognitive resources** — very high; creativity, strategic thinking, broad perspective.  
**Behavioural control** — strengthened; high resistance to stress and pressure.  
**Information processing** — fast, flexible, multi‑layered; able to integrate complex data.  
**Susceptibility to overload** — very low; stress is processed adaptively without loss of function.  
**Interpretation patterns** — constructive, grounded in agency and meaning.  
**Typical behaviours** — courage in pursuing goals, humour, self‑irony, high psychological resilience.  
**Metacognition** — expanded; strong self‑awareness, strategic self‑regulation, long‑term perspective.  
**Motivation / drive** — proactive; high initiative, intrinsic motivation, long‑range goal pursuit.  
**Characteristic cognitive biases** — constructive reframing, realistic optimism, high cognitive flexibility.  
**Gating profile** — optimally tuned; high‑bandwidth selective updating, rapid integration, minimal energetic cost.  
**Neuromodulatory signature** — endorphins differentiate CIR‑5 from CIR‑4 by adding analgesia, elevated mood, and increased resilience; dopamine and oxytocin are high, cortisol low, serotonin stable.  
**Memory bandwidth** — extended; enhanced by endorphin‑ and dopamine‑supported cognitive efficiency.  
**Functionality in the real world** — very high; the individual functions not only stably but creatively and proactively.

## CIR as a Multi‑Domain Regulatory Model

CIR models regulation as a dynamic system that explains a wide range of cognitive, emotional, behavioural, and neurobiological phenomena. Because each CIR Level corresponds to a distinct gating profile, neuromodulatory state, and resource‑allocation mode, the model can map both moment‑to‑moment shifts and long‑term regulatory patterns.

---

## How CIR explains relational and sensory interventions

CIR clarifies why interventions based on safety, connection, and sensory grounding are effective. At lower levels (CIR‑1 and CIR‑2), oxytocin is nearly absent and the system operates in a threat‑dominated mode. External relational cues—such as touch, supportive presence, animal‑assisted interaction, or attuned communication—trigger oxytocin release, stabilising gating and enabling upward transitions toward CIR‑3 and CIR‑4.

This provides a neuro‑computational explanation for the effectiveness of dog‑ and cat‑assisted therapy, supportive conversation, and close interpersonal contact.

---

## How CIR maps grief and sudden emotional drops

CIR captures the dynamics of grief by modelling how emotionally salient memories can rapidly destabilise regulation. A person functioning at CIR‑4 can drop directly to CIR‑2—bypassing CIR‑3—when reminded of a recent loss. The memory reactivates high‑load threat pathways, suppresses serotonin, and disrupts gating, pushing the system back into a collapse state.

This explains why grief can feel sudden, overwhelming, and disproportionate to the current context, even when the person was previously stable.

---

## How CIR maps burnout and long‑term regulatory erosion

CIR describes burnout as prolonged entrapment in CIR‑3, where regulation is maintained through high‑cost, forced gating. Over months or years, this state becomes the new baseline: cognitive flexibility decreases, emotional erosion progresses, and the system loses reserve capacity.

When the system can no longer sustain the energetic cost of CIR‑3, it collapses to CIR‑2—often the moment when burnout becomes visible to others. In severe cases, further collapse to CIR‑1 occurs, where regulation fails entirely.

---

## How CIR maps neurodiversity and atypical baselines

CIR accommodates neurodiversity by recognising that different systems have different default regulatory configurations. Some neuroatypical individuals naturally operate closer to CIR‑5, with high cognitive bandwidth, rapid integration, and strong top‑down modulation. Others may have baselines closer to CIR‑3 or CIR‑2 due to sensory load, gating variability, or neuromodulatory differences.

This makes CIR a flexible model that describes regulatory architecture, not traits or diagnoses.

---

## How CIR explains mixed or contradictory presentations

CIR can model complex regulatory patterns, such as a neuroatypical individual experiencing burnout. Such a person may be trapped in CIR‑2 due to overload, yet still exhibit sudden spikes to CIR‑5 because their system retains strong high‑bandwidth gating mechanisms.

These rapid oscillations can mask the underlying problem, creating a misleading picture of “sometimes euphoric, sometimes collapsed.” CIR frames this not as inconsistency but as a predictable interaction between baseline architecture and regulatory load.

---

## How CIR integrates cross‑domain interactions

CIR can combine multiple regulatory influences—stress, neurodiversity, grief, sensory load, social context—into a single dynamic map. Because each level corresponds to a specific neuromodulatory and gating profile, the model can explain:

- why someone collapses under chronic load,  
- why someone else rebounds quickly,  
- why certain interventions work only at specific levels,  
- why some systems oscillate while others degrade linearly,  
- why two people with similar external conditions diverge in regulatory trajectories.

This makes CIR a unified framework for modelling regulation across cognitive, emotional, behavioural, and neurobiological domains.

---

## Neurodiversity and regulatory dynamics in CIR

Neuroatypical systems often operate with a different baseline configuration of gating, neuromodulation, and cognitive bandwidth. CIR captures these differences by modelling how resource allocation and neuromodulatory cycles behave under load, allowing the full regulatory dynamics of neurodiversity to be represented within the same framework.

### Natural baselines closer to CIR‑5
Many neuroatypical individuals function with higher‑than‑average cognitive bandwidth, rapid integration, and strong top‑down modulation. This places their natural baseline closer to CIR‑5, where dopamine‑driven focus, high cognitive throughput, and efficient gating are common.

### Dopamine desensitisation and sensory overload
Sustained high‑dopamine states cannot be maintained indefinitely. Over time, dopaminergic channels undergo desensitisation, reducing responsiveness and destabilising gating. When this happens, the system becomes vulnerable to sensory overload, which CIR maps as a rapid descent through regulatory levels:

- **CIR‑3** — sensory overload emerges as gating becomes effortful and unstable.  
- **CIR‑2** — shutdown occurs when the system collapses into low‑bandwidth, protective gating.  
- **CIR‑1** — meltdown appears when inhibitory control fails and the system enters full overload.

This sequence is characteristic for many neuroatypical profiles and is captured naturally by CIR’s level transitions.

### Rapid upward spikes and masking effects
Neuroatypical systems often retain strong high‑bandwidth gating even when dysregulated. As a result, they may exhibit sudden spikes from CIR‑2 or CIR‑3 directly to CIR‑5, driven by bursts of dopamine, hyperfocus, or intense interest. These rapid transitions can mask underlying dysregulation, creating a misleading picture of “sometimes euphoric, sometimes collapsed,” even when the system is under chronic load.

### Cross‑domain interactions
CIR can model complex combinations such as neurodiversity plus burnout. A neuroatypical individual experiencing chronic overload may be trapped in CIR‑2 or CIR‑3, yet still show intermittent CIR‑5 spikes. This pattern often confuses observers, but within CIR it is a predictable interaction between baseline architecture, neuromodulatory cycles, and regulatory load.

### Representing Crisis‑Level Dysregulation
CIR can model extreme breakdown states in which regulatory mechanisms fail and the system enters high‑risk, threat‑dominated modes. These states may correspond, in real‑world contexts, to crisis‑level behavioural patterns driven by loss of inhibitory control, neuromodulatory overload, and collapse of executive access. Within CIR, such states are represented purely as mechanistic configurations of gating, resource allocation, and cognitive stability. The framework does not describe or predict specific behaviours and is not intended for clinical assessment, diagnosis, or therapeutic use.
